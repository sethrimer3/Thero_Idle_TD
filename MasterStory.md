# Project Thero — Prologue

The prologue is presented as journal entries written by **Slade Mercer** during the final days of Project Thero before the experiment that traps him inside the mindscape.

These entries are discovered gradually during the early levels of the game.

---

# Journal — Page 592

We finished the final entanglement requirements for the ℘ gate today. The **Weierstrass fractal** was the last key—its structure resonated perfectly with the measured frequency of consciousness.

It feels strange.

We’ve been working on this project for so long that reaching this point almost feels unreal. I find myself nostalgic for the earlier days—when we were younger, staying late into the night, slowly filling the lab fridge with caffeine and cheap food, making unspoken agreements that none of us would leave until something finally worked.

At the time it felt like chaos: scrambling to produce anything presentable so the funding board wouldn’t think the project was a mistake.

But those nights did something unexpected.

They bonded us.

Lab **C137** slowly stopped feeling like a workplace and started feeling like something closer to sacred ground.

I thought reaching this milestone would feel like arriving somewhere permanent—some grand destination that would satisfy me for the rest of my life.

Instead it feels more like the end of a chapter I didn’t realize I would miss.

And yet it isn’t over.

We still have weeks of stress testing ahead of us before the live trial begins, and after that there will be other projects, other discoveries.

Still, I can’t shake the feeling that this moment matters more than we realize.

Perhaps I’m worried about how easy it is, once a great thing is finished, to get distracted… and move on.

---

# Journal — Page 597

Matt ran the latest calibration cycle today and the MG stabilized far faster than we expected. Isaac thinks the signal drift we saw last week wasn’t noise at all, but the system beginning to synchronize with my baseline cognitive pattern.

Lydia is cautiously optimistic, which for her is the equivalent of throwing a parade.

The strange part is how natural the interface feels.

When the Gate initializes, it doesn’t feel like entering a machine. It feels more like stepping into an unfinished space that somehow already knows how to respond to attention.

I tried describing it to Tess earlier and she immediately launched into one of her lectures about relational frames and conceptual networks.

According to her, what I’m seeing might not be a “place” at all, but the brain externalizing how meaning connects to meaning.

According to me, if I can walk through it, build in it, and test structures in it, then it behaves like a place whether the philosophers approve or not.

We’ve argued about this for years.

Is mathematics something discovered in reality, or something invented by the mind?

If the Gate works the way Isaac believes it will, we might finally get an answer.

---

# Journal — Page 601

We pushed the interface further today than any previous run.

Matt let me stay inside the environment for nearly forty minutes before Lydia insisted we shut it down and run diagnostics. Florida sided with her immediately, which I suppose is the downside of having a physician present whenever I volunteer to be the test subject.

Still, something interesting happened during the run.

At one point the environment stabilized into a repeating geometric pattern I didn’t consciously construct. It resembled a fractured **Weierstrass curve** extending outward in every direction.

When I focused on it, the structure sharpened, almost like the system was waiting for confirmation that it should exist.

I asked Isaac afterward if the MG might be projecting mathematical objects directly from my cognition.

He said something unsettling.

He doesn’t think the system is projecting them.

He thinks the Gate may be **revealing them**.

Tess of course loved that idea. She spent the next hour arguing that the mindscape is probably composed of relational constructs rather than mathematical primitives.

We’re still having the same argument.

The difference now is that we’re arguing about it **inside a machine that might prove one of us wrong**.

---

# Journal — Page 604

Tomorrow is the first full immersion test.

We’ve completed every safety protocol Lydia demanded, every stress simulation Matt could imagine, and every neurological baseline Isaac insisted on collecting.

Florida has already warned me twice that she reserves the right to terminate the experiment if my vitals drift even slightly outside the acceptable range.

Tess stopped by the lab tonight after everyone else left.

We sat in C137 for a while, watching the MG run idle diagnostics. The Gate emits this faint oscillating tone when it’s powered, like distant glass vibrating.

I’ve heard it so often that the silence in my apartment feels wrong without it.

She asked me again whether I think mathematics exists independently of us.

I told her that if the mindscape contains structures I never consciously formed, then mathematics may be something deeper than human cognition.

She smiled and said if the structures only appear once a mind is there to interpret them, then meaning still comes first.

We’ve never managed to win that argument.

Maybe that’s why we keep having it.

Tomorrow I’ll be the first person to enter the Gate completely.

If everything works, the system should map the entire structure of my consciousness and allow controlled interaction with the cognitive environment.

If Isaac is right, we might be standing on the edge of the greatest discovery in the history of science.

And if Tess is right…

Then tomorrow we might discover that the mind is far stranger than any equation we’ve written.

Either way, I suppose I’ll find out soon enough.

# Project Thero — Chapter 1  
## Isolation

After the Mind Gate is stolen during the experiment, Slade awakens alone inside the mindscape.

With no communication from the outside world, he begins experimenting with the strange conceptual environment around him. The structures of the mindscape respond to mathematics, intention, and cognition.

As the chapter progresses, Slade slowly realizes the worst possibility:

The machine may no longer be in the lab.

---

# Level 1-1

At first I assumed the connection simply dropped.

The Gate had lost signal before during earlier tests. Usually Matt would restart the interface and Isaac would recalibrate the frequency, and within a few seconds the lab would appear again through the observation channel.

That hasn’t happened.

The environment is still stable. That means the Mind Geiger is still running somewhere.

But I can’t hear them.

No voices. No telemetry overlay. No diagnostic messages from Matt.

Just the quiet geometry of this place.

I tried initiating a recall protocol earlier. Nothing responded.

Which means either the system isn’t receiving my signals… or no one is there to answer them.

---

# Level 1-2

Time behaves strangely here.

I know Lydia warned that subjective duration inside the Gate might drift slightly compared to the lab clock, but this feels different. The environment doesn’t move unless I interact with it.

Structures appear when I focus on them.

Like equations waiting to be written.

If Isaac is right, then this space might be a representation of cognitive architecture.

Which would mean these shapes aren’t random.

They’re ideas.

Or at least the mathematical skeletons ideas use to exist.

That thought should be exciting.

Instead it makes the silence feel heavier.

Because if the Gate is still functioning, someone in the lab should be able to see everything I’m seeing.

And they still haven’t answered.

---

# Level 1-3

I’ve been experimenting.

If this place really is built from conceptual structures, then it should respond to mathematical rules. Earlier I tried stabilizing one of the collapsing geometries by applying a recursive constraint.

It worked.

The environment didn’t just react—it accepted the proof.

That’s the best way I can describe it.

Like the structure was waiting for someone to justify its existence.

If Tess were here she’d probably say this proves her point about meaning shaping cognition.

I’d say it proves something stranger.

If the Gate is truly externalizing my mind, then these structures shouldn’t surprise me.

But they do.

Which means either I don’t understand my own thoughts…

Or the Gate is revealing patterns deeper than individual cognition.

I’d give a lot right now to argue about that with someone.

---

# Level 1-4

I tried something different today.

Instead of forcing structures to behave, I waited.

At first nothing happened. Just the endless lattice of half-formed patterns drifting in the distance.

Then slowly the environment began organizing itself around a repeating curve.

It looked familiar.

After a few minutes I realized why.

It’s the same Weierstrass pattern we used during the Gate calibration.

The one that resonated with the frequency of consciousness.

If that structure exists here, then the MG isn’t just mapping my mind.

It’s mapping the interface between cognition and mathematics.

Which raises a more immediate question.

If the Gate is still active…

Where is everyone?

---

# Level 1-5

Something is wrong.

I’m sure of it now.

Even if the lab lost communication with the interface, the emergency recall should have triggered automatically.

Matt designed at least three redundant shutdown systems.

Lydia insisted on another two after that.

Yet I’m still here.

Which means one of two things has happened.

Either every single failsafe has failed at once…

Or the machine isn’t where we left it.

I tried sending another signal through the interface layer earlier.

Still nothing.

No response.

No interruption.

Just the quiet hum of a system that is clearly still running.

And the growing realization that whatever happened outside the Gate…

…it wasn’t part of the experiment.

---

# Level 1-6 — Story Level

At some point I stopped expecting the recall signal.

That realization came slowly.

The first few hours—days, maybe—were spent assuming Matt or Isaac would solve whatever technical issue had interrupted the interface. They built the system, after all. If anyone could restore the connection, it would be them.

But the Gate hasn’t shut down.

And the lab hasn’t answered.

I’ve replayed the moment before the signal vanished dozens of times in my head. There was no warning. No alarms. No indication that anything was about to go wrong.

One moment the professors were monitoring the run like always.

The next moment the channel went silent.

Which leaves a possibility I’ve been trying not to think about.

The machine might not be in the lab anymore.

If someone moved the Mind Geiger while the interface was active, the Gate would remain open as long as the system stayed powered. My consciousness would still be mapped inside the environment, but the people who built the system might not be the ones controlling it anymore.

I don’t know who would want something like the Mind Gate.

But if they have it…

They also have access to a machine capable of mapping and influencing human cognition.

I hope I’m wrong.

Because if I’m right, then the problem isn’t that my friends abandoned me.

The problem is that something happened in the lab.

And I’m the only one who doesn’t know what it was.

# Project Thero — Chapter 2
## First Contact

After a long silence, Slade finally detects fragments of communication within the mindscape.

Using salvaged equipment, the professors manage to re-establish a weak connection to the Mind Gate. At first the signals appear as unstable symbolic disturbances inside the conceptual architecture of the environment.

Gradually the transmissions stabilize.

For the first time since the experiment failed, Slade is no longer alone.

---

# Level 2-1

I’ve started organizing the structures here.

Calling them “structures” might not be accurate. They behave more like incomplete ideas that stabilize when attention lingers on them long enough.

The mathematician in me wants to treat this place like a proof waiting to be completed. Every pattern suggests another pattern. Every fragment implies a rule.

But the longer I stay here, the harder it becomes to ignore the obvious question.

Why hasn’t anyone pulled me out?

Matt would never leave a system running this long without checking the interface. Lydia would have shut the entire project down the moment the telemetry failed.

And Tess…

Tess would have found some way to argue with me through the silence.

I keep thinking the next structure I stabilize will trigger the recall signal.

It hasn’t yet.

---

# Level 2-2

I might be adapting to the environment.

That realization bothers me more than it should.

Earlier today I solved a stability problem without consciously thinking about the equation first. The solution simply appeared as a structure, and the environment accepted it immediately.

Either the Gate is learning from me…

or I’m learning from the Gate.

I’ve also noticed something else.

The deeper layers of the environment seem quieter. Less chaotic. Almost as if the mindscape becomes more coherent the further inward I move.

Which raises an unsettling possibility.

If this place reflects cognition itself, then maybe the surface is where uncertainty lives.

And the deeper levels are where meaning becomes… clearer.

I wonder if the professors can see any of this.

---

# Level 2-3

Something changed.

At first I thought it was just another environmental shift, but the pattern didn’t behave like the others. Instead of forming from my focus, it appeared suddenly—like a ripple passing through the entire structure of the mindscape.

Then I heard something.

Not a voice exactly.

More like a fragment of sound trying to resolve into language.

For a moment I thought I imagined it. But the pattern repeated again, this time sharper. A sequence of symbols appeared in the distance, flickering in and out of stability.

The structure didn’t belong to the environment.

It looked like… a transmission.

---

# Level 2-4

The signal stabilized for a few seconds.

Long enough to hear a voice.

Matt’s voice.

The message was fragmented, like it had been forced through layers of interference, but the words were unmistakable.

“—Slade, if you can see this, respond—”

Then the connection collapsed again.

I tried sending a signal back through the interface layer, but the channel flickered before I could finish forming the response.

Still.

That was Matt.

Which means the lab still exists.

And they’re trying to reach me.

---

# Level 2-5

The signal returned again.

This time longer.

Not clear, but recognizable.

Matt, Isaac, and Lydia were all speaking over each other while Matt tried to stabilize the transmission. I caught pieces of the conversation before the signal faded again.

“…we know he’s still active…”

“…frequency lock isn’t stable yet…”

“…don’t push the signal too hard or we lose the channel…”

And then Tess’s voice cut through the noise.

“Slade, if you can hear this, don’t move deeper yet. The structure changes the further you go.”

That sounded exactly like something she would say.

Half instruction.

Half argument.

For the first time since the connection vanished, I’m certain of something.

They’re still out there.

---

# Level 2-6 — Story Level

The signal stabilized long enough for an actual conversation.

Matt’s face appeared briefly inside the shifting geometry of the mindscape interface. The image flickered constantly, like the system was struggling to maintain synchronization between two different realities.

“Slade,” he said, sounding more relieved than I’ve ever heard him. “Good. That means the channel works.”

For a moment none of us spoke. It was strange seeing the lab again after so long—Matt leaning over the console, Lydia watching the diagnostics, Isaac standing behind them with that expression he gets when something impossible is suddenly real.

Tess was there too, just off to the side of the camera.

“What happened?” I asked.

Matt hesitated.

Isaac answered instead.

“The machine was taken.”

At first the words didn’t register.

“Taken?” I repeated.

“The funding group,” Lydia said quietly. “They arrived during the run. Security was already compromised. We tried to shut the Gate down, but the system was already fully synchronized with your consciousness.”

“They moved the MG while you were still inside it,” Matt added.

The implications settled slowly.

“You’re saying the Mind Gate isn’t in the lab anymore.”

Isaac nodded.

“We don’t know where it is now. But the system is still powered, which is why the environment hasn’t collapsed.”

Tess leaned closer to the camera.

“We’re working on a way to stabilize the communication channel,” she said. “Once we understand how the signal is passing through the interface, we might be able to help you navigate the structure from our side.”

I looked out across the endless patterns of the mindscape.

“So I’m stuck here.”

“For now,” Matt admitted.

“But not alone anymore,” Tess said.

For the first time since the connection vanished, the silence of the mindscape felt different.

Not empty.

Just distant.

And somewhere beyond that distance, the people who built this impossible machine were still trying to reach me.

# Project Thero — Chapter 3
## The Threshold

With communication stabilized, the professors begin working with Slade to understand the deeper architecture of the mindscape.

Together they attempt something far more ambitious than simple navigation.

They try to bring him home.

But during the final stages of the extraction process, they discover the stolen Mind Gate is already being used for something far more dangerous.

---

# Level 3-1

The communication channel is still unstable, but at least it exists now.

Matt managed to stabilize the signal long enough for us to exchange real data this morning. Apparently the system they’re using isn’t the original Mind Gate console anymore. Most of the lab equipment was confiscated when the funding group took the machine.

What they’re using now is something Isaac and Matt cobbled together from backup components and whatever Lydia could salvage before the security teams shut the lab down.

It shouldn’t work.

Matt says that a lot lately.

Still, the signal holds long enough for them to observe the structures forming around me. Isaac seems fascinated by how the environment reacts when I stabilize the patterns. He thinks the Gate isn’t just mapping my mind anymore.

He thinks the system is learning from it.

Tess says that sounds suspiciously like mathematics behaving like language.

I told her we can argue about that after I’m out of here.

---

# Level 3-2

Working together changes everything.

Before the signal returned, every problem here felt like guessing in the dark. Now Matt can watch the structures forming in real time and suggest adjustments.

Lydia even managed to map part of the interface where my cognitive pattern overlaps with the Gate’s hardware systems. Apparently that layer acts like a bridge between the biological and mathematical components of the machine.

She calls it the biochemical anchor.

I call it the reason my brain hasn’t melted yet.

Florida mostly monitors my neural stability while Isaac runs simulations on how the structures respond to different conceptual inputs. Tess, meanwhile, insists on translating half the patterns into linguistic diagrams before we implement them.

It’s chaotic.

But for the first time since the experiment began, it feels like the project again.

Like Lab C137 never stopped existing.

---

# Level 3-3

We might actually be able to do this.

Isaac believes he’s found a way to reverse the mapping process.

When the MG scanned my consciousness, it created a resonance profile unique to my cognitive structure. If Matt can generate a matching frequency from the outside, the system should be able to re-synchronize the Gate with my physical body.

In theory, that would allow the interface to reconstruct my consciousness back into normal neural activity.

In practice, it requires solving a small collection of problems.

First, they have to stabilize the transmission long enough to synchronize the resonance pattern.

Second, the environment here has to remain structurally stable during the transfer.

And third…

Well.

Third we need to hope the machine hasn’t been repurposed for something dangerous.

None of us said that part out loud.

---

# Level 3-4

The extraction process is closer than any of us expected.

Matt has been running synchronization tests through the channel all day, gradually adjusting the frequency until the signal lines up with the pattern Isaac calculated.

The strange part is how the environment reacts to those tests.

Every time they adjust the resonance outside the Gate, the mindscape shifts slightly. Patterns reorganize. Structures tighten. Entire regions of the environment seem to move closer together.

Tess says it looks like the system preparing for a translation.

Like the mindscape itself is waiting for the moment when my consciousness moves back into the physical world.

If she’s right, then the Gate was designed to do exactly this.

Which means if the synchronization works…

I might actually leave this place.

---

# Level 3-5

The synchronization test worked.

For a moment—just a moment—I felt the connection between the mindscape and my physical body snap into focus.

Not visually.

Something deeper than that.

Like suddenly remembering where your hands are after forgetting them.

Matt immediately cut the signal after the test, but Isaac confirmed what we all suspected.

The extraction protocol works.

All we have to do now is run the full synchronization cycle.

Tess asked me how I felt about leaving.

I told her the truth.

I’m ready to come home.

---

# Level 3-6 — Story Level

The full synchronization sequence began an hour later.

Matt counted down while Isaac monitored the resonance patterns and Lydia watched the neurological data streaming through the channel. The entire lab looked tense, even through the distortion of the transmission.

“Frequency alignment holding,” Isaac said.

“Biochemical anchor stable,” Lydia added.

Matt looked directly into the camera.

“Alright, Slade,” he said. “When the signal peaks, the system will pull your consciousness back through the Gate.”

The mindscape began shifting around me as the resonance built.

Structures folded inward. The environment tightened into a single converging point. It felt like gravity was suddenly pulling every idea in the system toward the same center.

Then Isaac spoke again.

But this time his voice sounded different.

“Matt… wait.”

The lab grew quiet.

“What is it?” Matt asked.

Isaac was staring at something off-screen.

“I’m seeing another signal in the MG network.”

Lydia leaned closer to the monitors.

“That’s not ours.”

Matt frowned.

“What do you mean?”

Isaac hesitated before answering.

“It looks like the machine is broadcasting.”

A pause.

Then Tess spoke quietly.

“Broadcasting where?”

Isaac turned back toward the camera.

“Everywhere.”

A new pattern appeared in the mindscape then—one I hadn’t created.

It spread through the environment like a shadow moving across the structures, distorting the patterns as it passed.

“What is that?” I asked.

Isaac looked pale.

“I think,” he said slowly, “the people who stole the machine aren’t just studying the mind anymore.”

“They’re trying to influence it.”

The extraction signal was still building.

Matt looked back at me.

“Slade,” he said carefully, “we can pull you out right now.”

Behind him, the distorted pattern continued spreading through the mindscape like a growing storm.

For the first time since entering the Gate, I wasn’t thinking about escape.

I was thinking about what would happen if I left.

And whatever the people controlling the Mind Gate were doing to the world outside…

They had just started.

# Project Thero — Chapter 4
## The Fires

Slade refuses extraction and remains inside the mindscape.

Together with the professors, he begins confronting the distortions spreading through the collective unconscious. These distortions represent archetypal patterns being broadcast into human cognition by the stolen Mind Gate technology.

Each one behaves like a spreading fire inside the conceptual architecture of the mindscape.

And for the first time, Slade understands the scale of the battlefield.

---

# Level 4-1

I told Matt to stop the extraction.

At first he thought I meant to delay it for another test. But when I explained what I was seeing in the mindscape—the spreading distortions, the structures bending toward patterns I never created—the lab fell quiet.

Isaac was the first to understand.

“They’re using the Gate to broadcast cognitive signals,” he said slowly. “Large-scale pattern injection.”

Lydia looked uneasy.

“You’re saying they’re influencing people’s thoughts?”

Isaac shook his head.

“Not thoughts.”

“Conditions.”

I looked out across the mindscape again. The spreading distortion had grown larger, infecting entire regions of the structure.

Whatever the stolen machine was doing, it wasn’t targeting individuals.

It was targeting everyone.

If I leave now, I thought, no one will be here to stop it.

So I told Matt to shut down the extraction sequence.

For a long moment, no one spoke.

Then Tess said quietly,

“I had a feeling you’d say that.”

---

# Level 4-2

Isaac thinks the distortions I’m seeing correspond to real-world broadcasts from the stolen MG.

The machine isn’t controlling people directly.

It’s injecting archetypal patterns into the cognitive layer shared by all human minds.

Subtle pressures.

Amplifications of fear.

Suspicion.

Anger.

Left alone, the effects might be small.

But repeated across millions of minds…

the outcome could reshape entire societies.

Matt compared it to introducing a tiny imbalance into a physical system. If the force is applied consistently enough, even stable structures eventually shift.

Which means the people controlling the machine might not need armies.

Just influence.

And if the mindscape truly reflects collective cognition…

then this place might be the battlefield.

---

# Level 4-3

The first “fire” appeared near the outer layers of the environment.

That’s the only word that fits.

The distortion wasn’t just spreading—it was feeding on unstable structures, twisting them into aggressive recursive patterns. Mathematical loops that collapsed into themselves and exploded outward again.

Isaac thinks those loops represent feedback cycles in real human populations.

Fear reinforcing fear.

Suspicion amplifying suspicion.

I stabilized the first region using the recursive constraints Matt suggested earlier. Once the structure held, the distortion lost momentum and collapsed back into noise.

It worked.

Which means this place isn’t just reflecting cognition.

It’s influencing it.

Tess called it a terrifying experiment in collective psychology.

I called it a responsibility.

---

# Level 4-4

More distortions are appearing.

Each one slightly different.

Some resemble unstable equations. Others look like symbolic constructs that Tess says resemble primitive narrative archetypes.

Hatred.

Paranoia.

Tribal identity.

Patterns that encourage people to see the world in simpler, more hostile ways.

The deeper I go into the mindscape, the clearer the structures become. Almost as if the environment is trying to reveal the foundations beneath those archetypes.

Isaac thinks I’m interacting with something close to what Jung called the collective unconscious.

If that’s true, then the people using the Mind Gate aren’t just manipulating individuals.

They’re rewriting cultural instincts.

---

# Level 4-5

The scale of the problem is becoming clearer.

Matt intercepted fragments of data from the stolen MG network earlier today. The signals are being broadcast through multiple transmission points now.

Not just one machine.

Several.

Which means the group that stole Project Thero didn’t keep the technology to themselves.

They distributed it.

Isaac suspects multiple governments are already experimenting with the system.

The distortions appearing in the mindscape might be the result of competing broadcasts interfering with each other.

A kind of arms race.

Except the battlefield isn’t territory.

It’s human perception.

---

# Level 4-6 — Story Level

Matt looked exhausted when the transmission stabilized again.

“How many?” I asked.

Isaac answered instead.

“Three confirmed machines. Possibly more.”

Lydia shook her head slowly.

“We built a research device,” she said. “How did it turn into this?”

“Because someone realized what it could do,” Isaac replied.

Tess leaned closer to the camera.

“Slade, the distortions you’re stabilizing… we’re starting to see changes in the external data.”

“What kind of changes?” I asked.

“Small ones,” she said. “Nothing dramatic yet. But regions where the signals weaken are also showing decreased volatility in behavioral analytics.”

Matt nodded.

“If what we’re seeing is real, then stabilizing structures inside the mindscape is dampening the broadcasts.”

I looked out across the endless layers of the environment.

New distortions were forming on the horizon.

“How many fires can we put out?” I asked.

Isaac hesitated.

“We don’t know.”

The silence that followed felt heavier than any equation.

Finally Tess spoke again.

“You understand what this means, right?”

I did.

As long as the cognitive war continues, someone has to remain inside the system to counter it.

Which means the decision I made earlier might not just delay my return.

It might prevent it entirely.

I took a slow breath.

“Then we’d better get good at this.”

Because somewhere in the world, someone had turned human consciousness into a weapon.

And for now…

the only place to fight that war was here.

# Project Thero — Chapter 5
## The Hunt

Slade’s interference with the cognitive broadcasts does not go unnoticed.

The groups using the stolen Mind Gate technology quickly realize that someone inside the mindscape is counteracting their influence. They begin searching for the source of the interference.

At the same time, the professors who created Project Thero become targets themselves.

The world outside the mindscape begins closing in.

---

# Level 5-1

The distortions have changed again.

Earlier they behaved like uncontrolled fires—spreading through unstable conceptual regions and amplifying whatever psychological pattern happened to resonate with them.

Now they feel deliberate.

The structures forming around the distortions resemble probes, or perhaps search patterns. Instead of expanding randomly, they move through the architecture of the mindscape with careful precision, touching structures, testing them, then moving on.

Isaac noticed the shift before I did.

“They’re mapping the interference,” he said through the transmission channel.

“Trying to find the source.”

Which means they’ve realized something inside the system is pushing back.

Something—or someone.

---

# Level 5-2

The professors think my resonance signature is visible from outside the system.

When the Mind Gate mapped my consciousness, it created a pattern unique to my cognitive structure. Isaac calls it a “wavelength of consciousness,” though he admits the phrase is more poetic than scientific.

If the groups controlling the stolen machines can detect that wavelength, they might be able to trace the disruptions in the mindscape back to me.

I asked Isaac what happens if they succeed.

He didn’t answer right away.

Eventually he said, “They would try to enter the system.”

“Using the Gate?” I asked.

“Using any interface they can build.”

Matt looked uneasy when Isaac said that.

Because the moment someone else steps into the mindscape…

I won’t be the only mind here anymore.

---

# Level 5-3

The manhunt started three days ago.

Not inside the mindscape.

Outside.

Matt explained it during the last stable transmission. The funding organization that originally supported Project Thero has begun quietly contacting universities and research groups connected to our work.

They’re asking questions about the Mind Gate.

About the professors who built it.

About me.

Isaac believes they suspect someone from the original research team is interfering with the broadcasts. They don’t know how or why yet, but they know the disruptions started shortly after the machine was stolen.

Which means eventually they’ll trace the interference back to the people who created the system.

Back to Matt.

Back to Lydia.

Back to Tess.

Back to all of them.

---

# Level 5-4

The lab is gone.

Matt delivered the news carefully, as if softening the words would somehow change them.

Security teams arrived at the university early yesterday morning. Officially they were investigating “misuse of experimental cognitive technology.” Unofficially they were confiscating anything remotely connected to Project Thero.

The MG interface consoles were seized first.

Then the servers.

Then the research archives.

Lydia managed to wipe several datasets before the authorities reached the storage clusters, but most of the equipment is gone now.

The only reason they still have a communication channel to the mindscape is because Matt and Isaac built a makeshift interface from backup components they had hidden off-site months ago.

Even that connection isn’t stable anymore.

And worse—

My physical body is no longer in the lab.

They moved me.

---

# Level 5-5

The cabin belongs to Matt’s family.

Apparently his grandfather built it decades ago in a stretch of forest so remote that even the nearest town barely appears on satellite maps. When the authorities began confiscating equipment from the lab, the professors realized the safest place for my body wasn’t anywhere near the university.

So they moved me in the middle of the night.

Isaac described the trip while the transmission struggled to hold together. They transported the life-support equipment in pieces, assembling it again inside the cabin once they arrived.

Florida now monitors my biological stability almost entirely alone.

Matt and Lydia are constantly moving equipment, hiding anything that could reveal the cabin’s location.

And the communication channel to the mindscape—

the only link between me and the outside world—

is degrading.

Isaac says the system might not survive much longer.

---

# Level 5-6 — Story Level

The final transmission began with silence.

For several seconds the screen showed only static as Matt struggled to stabilize the signal. When the image finally resolved, the lab behind him was gone. Instead I saw wooden walls, a cluttered table, and equipment stacked in tight rows around the small room.

The cabin.

“We don’t have much time,” Isaac said immediately.

“Authorities are closing in on anyone connected to the project.”

Lydia leaned into view beside him.

“No,” Tess replied.

She stepped into the camera’s view, looking far more determined than the others.

“Not goodbye.”

“Tess—”

Isaac frowned.

# Project Thero — Chapter 6
## The Vigil

With Tess now inside the mindscape, Slade is no longer alone in the cognitive realm.

Together they begin confronting the increasingly aggressive distortions created by competing Mind Gate broadcasts. But the groups controlling the technology have also realized someone is resisting their influence.

Soon other minds begin entering the system.

The final defense of the mindscape begins.

# Level 6-1

Tess adapted to the mindscape faster than I expected.

I should have predicted that.

While I spent weeks learning how the environment responded to mathematical stability, Tess approached it like language. Instead of forcing structures into equations, she treated them like symbols—meanings waiting to be interpreted.

“Look at this,” she said during our first attempt to stabilize a distortion together.

The pattern looked like a broken recursive structure, similar to the feedback loops I had already learned to collapse.

But Tess didn’t try to destroy it.

She reframed it.

The moment she did, the entire structure shifted. The recursion stabilized, transforming from a destructive loop into something almost elegant.

“Meaning,” she said, “is just another kind of structure.”

I folded my arms.

“That sounds suspiciously like you’re claiming mathematics depends on cognition.”

She grinned.

“And that sounds suspiciously like you’re starting an argument.”

For the first time since entering the Gate, the mindscape didn’t feel lonely.

---

# Level 6-2

Working together changed the entire battlefield.

Where I stabilize the environment mathematically, Tess reshapes it through interpretation. A pattern I would collapse, she might reinterpret, transforming its function instead of destroying it.

Isaac calls it conceptual refactoring.

Matt calls it terrifying.

But the results are undeniable.

Regions of the mindscape that once required constant stabilization now maintain their structure naturally after Tess reinterprets their symbolic foundations.

“Language shapes cognition,” she said while we worked.

“And cognition shapes mathematics,” I replied.

She raised an eyebrow.

“Careful, Slade. That almost sounded like agreement.”

# Level 6-3

The first hostile mind entered the system without warning.

At first I thought it was another distortion, but the pattern behaved differently. It wasn’t chaotic. It moved deliberately through the structures of the mindscape, testing their stability the same way I had during my first days here.

Then it noticed us.

The entire environment tightened around the presence like a predator focusing on prey.

“Someone else is here,” I said.

Tess studied the structure carefully.

“Not someone,” she corrected.

“Something like us.”

Isaac confirmed it moments later through the fading communication channel.

“They’ve built their own interface.”

Which means the cognitive war has a new front.

Not just broadcasts.

Participants.

# Level 6-4

More agents followed.

Each one behaved differently inside the mindscape. Some manipulated structures with brute mathematical force, collapsing regions of stability to generate distortions faster than we could repair them.

Others used symbolic constructs similar to Tess’s methods, twisting archetypal narratives into aggressive psychological feedback loops.

It was like watching entire academic disciplines weaponized.

Isaac believes the people entering the system come from research teams studying cognition, artificial intelligence, or behavioral systems.

Which means they understand the environment almost as well as we do.

The difference is simple.

They’re trying to control it.

We’re trying to protect it.

# Level 6-5

The last transmission from the outside world arrived today.

Matt’s face appeared on the unstable interface channel, thinner than before but still carrying the same calm certainty he always had in the lab.

“We can’t keep the connection open anymore,” he said.

“The authorities have expanded the search.”

But if people become aware that such influence exists…

Isaac nodded.

“So we’re going to tell people.”

Years passed.

At least they did for us.

Time in the mindscape never behaved like normal time, but the gradual changes were impossible to ignore. The distortions appeared less frequently as more people in the outside world learned about cognitive manipulation and how to recognize it.

Entire regions of the mindscape stabilized permanently.

Hostile agents still appeared from time to time, but their influence weakened as the broadcasts lost power.

Eventually even the agents stopped coming.

Human cognition had changed.

Not dramatically. Not overnight.

Suspicion weakened.

Fear loops collapsed.

Narratives built on hostility lost their resonance.

Not violently.

Quietly.

The Mind Gate.

Carefully.

Quietly.

Without interference.

Older.

Matt.
