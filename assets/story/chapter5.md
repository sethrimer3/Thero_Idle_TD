# Project Thero — Chapter 5
## The Hunt

Slade’s interference with the cognitive broadcasts does not go unnoticed.

The groups using the stolen Mind Gate technology quickly realize that someone inside the mindscape is counteracting their influence. They begin searching for the source of the interference.

At the same time, the professors who created Project Thero become targets themselves.

The world outside the mindscape begins closing in.

---

# Level 5-1

The distortions have changed again.

Earlier they behaved like uncontrolled fires—spreading through unstable conceptual regions and amplifying whatever psychological pattern happened to resonate with them.

Now they feel deliberate.

The structures forming around the distortions resemble probes, or perhaps search patterns. Instead of expanding randomly, they move through the architecture of the mindscape with careful precision, touching structures, testing them, then moving on.

Isaac noticed the shift before I did.

“They’re mapping the interference,” he said through the transmission channel.

“Trying to find the source.”

Which means they’ve realized something inside the system is pushing back.

Something—or someone.

---

# Level 5-2

The professors think my resonance signature is visible from outside the system.

When the Mind Gate mapped my consciousness, it created a pattern unique to my cognitive structure. Isaac calls it a “wavelength of consciousness,” though he admits the phrase is more poetic than scientific.

If the groups controlling the stolen machines can detect that wavelength, they might be able to trace the disruptions in the mindscape back to me.

I asked Isaac what happens if they succeed.

He didn’t answer right away.

Eventually he said, “They would try to enter the system.”

“Using the Gate?” I asked.

“Using any interface they can build.”

Matt looked uneasy when Isaac said that.

Because the moment someone else steps into the mindscape…

I won’t be the only mind here anymore.

---

# Level 5-3

The manhunt started three days ago.

Not inside the mindscape.

Outside.

Matt explained it during the last stable transmission. The funding organization that originally supported Project Thero has begun quietly contacting universities and research groups connected to our work.

They’re asking questions about the Mind Gate.

About the professors who built it.

About me.

Isaac believes they suspect someone from the original research team is interfering with the broadcasts. They don’t know how or why yet, but they know the disruptions started shortly after the machine was stolen.

Which means eventually they’ll trace the interference back to the people who created the system.

Back to Matt.

Back to Lydia.

Back to Tess.

Back to all of them.

---

# Level 5-4

The lab is gone.

Matt delivered the news carefully, as if softening the words would somehow change them.

Security teams arrived at the university early yesterday morning. Officially they were investigating “misuse of experimental cognitive technology.” Unofficially they were confiscating anything remotely connected to Project Thero.

The MG interface consoles were seized first.

Then the servers.

Then the research archives.

Lydia managed to wipe several datasets before the authorities reached the storage clusters, but most of the equipment is gone now.

The only reason they still have a communication channel to the mindscape is because Matt and Isaac built a makeshift interface from backup components they had hidden off-site months ago.

Even that connection isn’t stable anymore.

And worse—

My physical body is no longer in the lab.

They moved me.

---

# Level 5-5

The cabin belongs to Matt’s family.

Apparently his grandfather built it decades ago in a stretch of forest so remote that even the nearest town barely appears on satellite maps. When the authorities began confiscating equipment from the lab, the professors realized the safest place for my body wasn’t anywhere near the university.

So they moved me in the middle of the night.

Isaac described the trip while the transmission struggled to hold together. They transported the life-support equipment in pieces, assembling it again inside the cabin once they arrived.

Florida now monitors my biological stability almost entirely alone.

Matt and Lydia are constantly moving equipment, hiding anything that could reveal the cabin’s location.

And the communication channel to the mindscape—

the only link between me and the outside world—

is degrading.

Isaac says the system might not survive much longer.

---

# Level 5-6 — Story Level

The final transmission began with silence.

For several seconds the screen showed only static as Matt struggled to stabilize the signal. When the image finally resolved, the lab behind him was gone. Instead I saw wooden walls, a cluttered table, and equipment stacked in tight rows around the small room.

The cabin.

“We don’t have much time,” Isaac said immediately.

“Authorities are closing in on anyone connected to the project.”

Lydia leaned into view beside him.

“They’ve confiscated most of the equipment we were using to communicate with you,” she said. “What you’re seeing now is probably the last stable connection we’ll have for a while.”

I felt the mindscape shift uneasily around me.

“So this is goodbye,” I said.

“No,” Tess replied.

She stepped into the camera’s view, looking far more determined than the others.

“Not goodbye.”

Matt looked confused.

“Tess—”

“I’ve been thinking about the problem,” she continued. “If Slade can’t leave the mindscape, then we’re solving the wrong equation.”

Isaac frowned.

“What equation should we be solving?”

Tess smiled faintly.

“The one where he isn’t alone.”

For a moment no one spoke.

Then Matt’s eyes widened.

“You’re not suggesting—”

“Yes,” Tess said.

Isaac shook his head immediately.

“That’s incredibly dangerous.”

“So was mapping a human consciousness into an experimental machine,” Tess replied calmly.

“Yet here we are.”

The connection flickered as Lydia adjusted the transmission hardware.

“Tess,” she said quietly, “once you enter the system, we might not be able to bring you back either.”

“I know.”

Tess looked directly into the camera then.

“Besides,” she said softly, “someone needs to keep arguing with him about whether mathematics exists independently of human thought.”

The screen flashed white.

For a moment the entire mindscape trembled, as if the structure of the environment had been struck by lightning.

Then a new resonance appeared.

Not a distortion.

Not a hostile signal.

A consciousness.

A figure slowly formed within the shifting geometry of the mindscape, stabilizing as the environment adjusted around it.

Tess looked around the endless patterns, studying the structures with the same curiosity she always showed in the lab.

“Well,” she said finally.

“You were right about one thing.”

I crossed my arms.

“Oh?”

She smiled slightly.

“It definitely behaves like a place.”
