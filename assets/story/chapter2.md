# Project Thero — Chapter 2
## First Contact

After a long silence, Slade finally detects fragments of communication within the mindscape.

Using salvaged equipment, the professors manage to re-establish a weak connection to the Mind Gate. At first the signals appear as unstable symbolic disturbances inside the conceptual architecture of the environment.

Gradually the transmissions stabilize.

For the first time since the experiment failed, Slade is no longer alone.

---

# Level 2-1

I’ve started organizing the structures here.

Calling them “structures” might not be accurate. They behave more like incomplete ideas that stabilize when attention lingers on them long enough.

The mathematician in me wants to treat this place like a proof waiting to be completed. Every pattern suggests another pattern. Every fragment implies a rule.

But the longer I stay here, the harder it becomes to ignore the obvious question.

Why hasn’t anyone pulled me out?

Matt would never leave a system running this long without checking the interface. Lydia would have shut the entire project down the moment the telemetry failed.

And Tess…

Tess would have found some way to argue with me through the silence.

I keep thinking the next structure I stabilize will trigger the recall signal.

It hasn’t yet.

---

# Level 2-2

I might be adapting to the environment.

That realization bothers me more than it should.

Earlier today I solved a stability problem without consciously thinking about the equation first. The solution simply appeared as a structure, and the environment accepted it immediately.

Either the Gate is learning from me…

or I’m learning from the Gate.

I’ve also noticed something else.

The deeper layers of the environment seem quieter. Less chaotic. Almost as if the mindscape becomes more coherent the further inward I move.

Which raises an unsettling possibility.

If this place reflects cognition itself, then maybe the surface is where uncertainty lives.

And the deeper levels are where meaning becomes… clearer.

I wonder if the professors can see any of this.

---

# Level 2-3

Something changed.

At first I thought it was just another environmental shift, but the pattern didn’t behave like the others. Instead of forming from my focus, it appeared suddenly—like a ripple passing through the entire structure of the mindscape.

Then I heard something.

Not a voice exactly.

More like a fragment of sound trying to resolve into language.

For a moment I thought I imagined it. But the pattern repeated again, this time sharper. A sequence of symbols appeared in the distance, flickering in and out of stability.

The structure didn’t belong to the environment.

It looked like… a transmission.

---

# Level 2-4

The signal stabilized for a few seconds.

Long enough to hear a voice.

Matt’s voice.

The message was fragmented, like it had been forced through layers of interference, but the words were unmistakable.

“—Slade, if you can see this, respond—”

Then the connection collapsed again.

I tried sending a signal back through the interface layer, but the channel flickered before I could finish forming the response.

Still.

That was Matt.

Which means the lab still exists.

And they’re trying to reach me.

---

# Level 2-5

The signal returned again.

This time longer.

Not clear, but recognizable.

Matt, Isaac, and Lydia were all speaking over each other while Matt tried to stabilize the transmission. I caught pieces of the conversation before the signal faded again.

“…we know he’s still active…”

“…frequency lock isn’t stable yet…”

“…don’t push the signal too hard or we lose the channel…”

And then Tess’s voice cut through the noise.

“Slade, if you can hear this, don’t move deeper yet. The structure changes the further you go.”

That sounded exactly like something she would say.

Half instruction.

Half argument.

For the first time since the connection vanished, I’m certain of something.

They’re still out there.

---

# Level 2-6 — Story Level

The signal stabilized long enough for an actual conversation.

Matt’s face appeared briefly inside the shifting geometry of the mindscape interface. The image flickered constantly, like the system was struggling to maintain synchronization between two different realities.

“Slade,” he said, sounding more relieved than I’ve ever heard him. “Good. That means the channel works.”

For a moment none of us spoke. It was strange seeing the lab again after so long—Matt leaning over the console, Lydia watching the diagnostics, Isaac standing behind them with that expression he gets when something impossible is suddenly real.

Tess was there too, just off to the side of the camera.

“What happened?” I asked.

Matt hesitated.

Isaac answered instead.

“The machine was taken.”

At first the words didn’t register.

“Taken?” I repeated.

“The funding group,” Lydia said quietly. “They arrived during the run. Security was already compromised. We tried to shut the Gate down, but the system was already fully synchronized with your consciousness.”

“They moved the MG while you were still inside it,” Matt added.

The implications settled slowly.

“You’re saying the Mind Gate isn’t in the lab anymore.”

Isaac nodded.

“We don’t know where it is now. But the system is still powered, which is why the environment hasn’t collapsed.”

Tess leaned closer to the camera.

“We’re working on a way to stabilize the communication channel,” she said. “Once we understand how the signal is passing through the interface, we might be able to help you navigate the structure from our side.”

I looked out across the endless patterns of the mindscape.

“So I’m stuck here.”

“For now,” Matt admitted.

“But not alone anymore,” Tess said.

For the first time since the connection vanished, the silence of the mindscape felt different.

Not empty.

Just distant.

And somewhere beyond that distance, the people who built this impossible machine were still trying to reach me.
